About & the mechanism — the story first, then the rules

Three layers, one on top of the other. The foundation: Linear Leios, the Ouroboros extension that gives every block round two bodies — a small instant one and a big certified one. The core of this prototype: dynamic pricing, a live per-byte price for each of those two lanes, enforced by the ledger. And on top, the simulation: a crowd of senders producing transactions to exercise the core and evidence delivery, live. The overview below shows the whole thing at a glance; the three sections after it detail one layer each — with examples that pick up today’s live quotes so they stay honest.

Overview — what am I looking at?

One flat fee treats a swap that must settle now and an overnight batch as the same customer — so under load they queue behind each other, and both overpay or both stall. Two-lane dynamic pricing gives the chain two lanes with live, demand-driven prices: pay for speed only when you need it, let patient traffic ride nearly free, and let each price breathe with its own lane's demand.

Urgent · the fast lane

Time-critical transactions, riding the ranking block forged every round. Space is scarce, so its quote climbs fast under load — and eases back as the lane clears. When the small block overflows, the remainder rides the next endorser block as riders — and pays only the standard quote on that slower delivery.

Standard · the patient lane

Routine traffic that trades a short delay for a far lower rate, riding the endorser block — ~133× the space, applied once the committee certifies it. Its price rests near the floor unless the lane genuinely fills.

💰 Where the money goes

The fee a sender signs is a max fee — a ceiling, not a price. The ledger charges the live quote of the block that actually delivers you — an urgent rider arriving in a certified endorser block pays only the standard quote. The base stays in the fee pot, the premium is donated to the treasury, the rest is refunded. The 💰 Incentives panel measures all three pots live.

Ouroboros extension Linear Leios — the consensus this prototype builds on: two blocks per round

One round, two blocks — the consensus upgrade underneath everything

On Cardano today (Praos), a round forges one ~90 KB block and that's the whole throughput budget. Linear Leios keeps the same rhythm but lets each round carry two bodies: the usual small ranking block, applied to the ledger the moment it arrives, and a big endorser block that is applied later, only after a committee of pools certifies it. Over a hundred times the bytes per round (the endorser block alone carries ~133× a ranking block), without touching the clock. One refinement: an endorser block is only issued when it is worth certifying — at least half a ranking block full; below that, the patient lane simply pools its transactions for the next round (the live cards show it as “pooling — below the announcement threshold”).

Capacity is measured in bytes, not tx. Max tx size is 16,384 bytes. The demo can pad its transactions (fat ≈ 2,300-byte tx → a full RB ≈ 40 tx, congestion with small counts) or send bare mainnet-size payments (≈ 210 bytes → a full RB ≈ 430 tx). The RB is applied first, then the certified EB — no filler between the two.

Certification — how the big block earns its place

The endorser block ships optimistically: before the whole network has checked it. The Leios rule: it only becomes part of the ledger once pools holding at least 75% of the stake vote for it. On this devnet the committee is the same three pools that forge — each holds ⅓ of the stake, so all three votes are needed. (Prototype honesty note: the votes are really cast and traced, but the certificate itself is still a simulated stub — the quorum arithmetic isn't wired in yet. The ⚡ certification miss scenario withholds the votes and the certificate together.)

And when the votes don't come? Only one endorser block is in flight at a time, so each one that is forged ends in exactly one of three states — the same colours the block diary uses. (A round can also issue no endorser block at all: below the announcement threshold the standard lane pools instead.)

certified — the normal case, usually at the next block (the protocol enforces a gap of at least 10 slots before the certificate can land).
superseded — never certified, but a later EB was: the old one is discarded and its txs are offered back to every mempool. What still clears the quote rides a later EB; what the climbed price refuses is counted in the journal as priced out on return.
stalled — no later EB certifies either: the whole patient lane freezes until votes return (the ⚡ certification miss scenario shows this live).

The network running it — three equal pools, one entry point

Three block-producing nodes, each with exactly ⅓ of the stake (30M ₳ delegated apiece), so the Praos lottery hands each about a third of the blocks — the diary and tooltips show which node forged each one. Every simulated tx enters at node1; the other nodes pull it from there (each hop re-validates it at that node's current prices). The certification committee is those same three pools.

Notice what's missing: a price. Linear Leios says where a tx travels and when it's applied — but not who gets in when more tx show up than the bytes can hold, nor why anyone would take the slower lane. That's the gap dynamic pricing fills.

Core feature Dynamic pricing — what this prototype adds: a live per-byte price for each lane

Two lanes with different service — instant vs certified-a-bit-later — can't share one flat fee: everyone would pile into the fast lane and the big cheap block would run empty. So the ledger publishes a separate quote (a price per byte) for each lane, and recomputes both after every block. Each tx declares its lane and a max fee — the most it's willing to pay. Fast lane crowded? Its quote climbs and the patient lane becomes a bargain. That price gap is the whole mechanism.

The self-correcting loop — how a price finds its level

No one sets these prices by hand. Each lane runs a tiny controller that watches how full its blocks are and nudges the price toward a target of half-full. That makes a loop — the highlighted step is roughly where the urgent lane is right now. The patient lane runs the same loop but almost never leaves the first step at realistic traffic — a 12 MB block is hard to half-fill, so its quote rests at the floor and the loop's drama plays out in the fast lane.

The repricing rule — what the ledger computes after every block

After every block, the ledger looks at how full that lane's block ran and republishes the price. That's the whole rule:

📈 Ran more than half-full → the price rises — the fuller it ran, the bigger the step, up to +6.25%.
📉 Ran less than half-full → the price falls — the emptier, the bigger the cut, down to −6.25%.
🧱 It never drops below the floor: 44 lovelace/byte.

Urgent · fast lane
Fullness is measured against its own block: the ranking block's 90,112 bytes. A small block fills easily, so this is the price that does the moving — the drama on the History chart is almost always this line.
Standard · patient lane
Same rule against its own 12,000,000-byte budget (~133× bigger). Each lane reads its fullness over a window of recent rounds (5 samples for the fast lane, 20 for this one — the CIP's signals), so one odd block cannot swing the price. Full EBs counted → +6.25% steps that compound; ranking-block rounds weigh the window down. A full pool climbs the staircase; a trickle decays to the floor.

And that is the whole rule: the two lanes are priced independently — no cross-lane floor. The urgent quote can even dip below the standard one for a round or two (a permitted state, not a bug); the experiments rejected fixed floors because they overprice the fast lane exactly when capacity is slack.

The fee split — who receives what a sender pays

A sender writes one number into their transaction: the max fee. The ledger charges the live quote of the block that delivers it — the urgent quote in the ranking block, the standard quote when it arrives as a rider in a certified endorser block — and splits the max fee three ways, conserving every lovelace — base + premium + refund = max fee:

🏗 Base — the protocol minimum fee — stays in the fee pot: the floor every transaction contributes to the operators, however cheap its lane. At the epoch boundary the treasury first takes its τ cut of the reward pot, and the rest pays the pools and their delegators.
🏛 Premium — quote − base, what congestion pricing charges above the minimum — is donated to the treasury: scarcity revenue funds the commons instead of inflating one block producer's luck.
↩️ Refund — max fee − quote, the unused headroom — is credited back to the transaction's refund account after the block. The max fee is a ceiling, not a price: this demo's senders cap at the live quote × their fee buffer (a cockpit slider), and the refund is whatever of that headroom the delivering block's quote leaves unspent — the full buffer margin when the price held still, less when it climbed while the tx waited, plus the whole urgent-to-standard drop for a rider delivered by a certified endorser block.

The catch worth knowing: the split only runs for a transaction that names a refund account (a registered staking account — this demo uses one of the devnet's genesis delegators). A transaction without one pays its whole max fee into the fee pot — no premium, no refund. And a refund owed to an unregistered account parks in the pending pot forever: one of the open questions the prototype keeps on the table.

Simulation The sender crowd — transactions produced to exercise the core and evidence delivery
How this demo is driven — what's real, what's simulated

The system under test is live. Three Cardano Dijkstra nodes run Linear Leios, forge the protocol's blocks and pool votes, and execute the ledger pricing controller. Every quote, queue depth, block and trace shown here comes from that running three-pool network.

The demand is generated synthetically. Rather than wait for real users, a load generator feeds the network through node1 with a synthetic stream of payments (the other two nodes pull them from there). This run uses the actor mode: for each payment it samples a value and an urgency, reads the two live quotes, and picks the lane that keeps more value after the fee — urgent if speed is worth it, standard if it can wait, or walks away if neither pays off. So the crowd reacts to the prices exactly like real senders would. You can reshape that crowd with the scenarios in the 📊 Live cockpit.

You can add trouble on demand. On top of the regular crowd, the 🎛 Drive the demand cockpit (top of the 📊 Live view) has a switch that turns a pressure scenario on and off live: a price squeeze (a burst of urgent payments capped just above today's price, whose queued backlog gets dropped as the quote climbs past their max fees), a coin clash (pairs of payments fighting over the same coin — the late-comer is turned away), or a certification miss (the committee goes silent and the whole patient lane stalls until it returns). Switch one on, watch the drops stream in; switch it off, watch them fade.

The mempool's rules of the game: a transaction is admitted only if its max fee covers the current quote — for urgent senders the higher of the two, since delivery may come through either block; while it waits, a rising quote can evict it (priced out); a coin conflict is settled first-come — the transaction admitted first keeps the coin, the newcomer is turned away; an endorser block only counts once the committee certifies it; and it is only issued when its payload — the patient queue merged first-come with the urgent overflow — carries at least half a ranking block; below that the round pools for the next one.

💰 Incentives — where the money goes — the ledger's own pots, measured each block

▾

Every transaction pays its max fee up front, and the ledger splits it three ways: the base fee stays in the fee pot, the premium above it is donated to the treasury, and the unused headroom flows back to the sender's account as a refund. These counters are read from the node's ledger state once per block — measured pots, not a model.

How the senders set their max fee: each transaction caps at its lane's live quote × the fee buffer (the cockpit slider — headroom against being priced out while waiting), capped by the demo's FEE ceiling. The refund is that buffer margin coming back once the going rate is charged — plus, for urgent riders delivered by a certified endorser block, the drop from the urgent quote to the standard one. Set the buffer to 0 in the cockpit to restore the old flat ceilings and watch the refunds balloon.
